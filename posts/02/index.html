Summary and highlights: The Daily Stoic by Ryan Holiday and Stephen Hanselman - Introduction & Part 1

Posted on Jan 31, 2023

Introduction

  • Stoicism is often misunderstood
  • Definition of Stoicism: ancient philosophy that was once one of the most popular civic disciplines in the west
  • Contain some of the greatest wisdom in the history of the world - referring to Meditations by Marcus Aurelius: a series of private notes kept by the Roman Emperor
  • The objective of the book The Daily Stoic is to restore Stoicism to its rightful place as a tool in the pursuit of self-mastery, perseverance and wisdom
  • The philosophy asserts that virtue (the four cardinal virtues of self-control, courage, justice and wisdom) is happiness, and it is our perceptions of things rather than the things themselves that cause most of our trouble
  • It teaches that we can’t control or rely on anything outside of our “reasoned choice” - our ability to choose how we categorise, respond and reorient ourselves to external events
  • Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus and Seneca framed their work revolving Stoicism around a series of exercises in three critical disciplines:
    • The Discipline of Perception - how we see and perceive the world around us
    • The Discipline of Action - the decisions and actions we take and to what end
    • The Discipline of Will - how we deal with the things we cannot change, attain clear and convincing judgement and come to a true understanding of our place in the world
  • By controlling our perceptions, the Stoics tell us we can find mental clarity - in directing our actions properly and justly, we will be effective
  • The ares of great interest to the Stoics all make an appearance here: virtue, mortality, emotions, self-awareness, fortitude, right action, problem solving, acceptance, mental clarity, pragmatism, unbiased thought and duty

January - Clarity

01/01: Control and choice

  • the most important practice in Stoic philosophy is differentiating between what we can change and what we can’t
  • If we can focus on making clear what parts of our day are within our control and what parts are not, we will not only be happier, we will have a distinct advantage over other people who fail to realise they are fighting an unwinnable battle

02/01: Education is freedom

  • reading and meditation on the wisdom of great minds is not to be done for its own sake. It has purpose

03/01: Be ruthless to the things that don’t matter

  • The more you say no to the things that don’t matter, the more you can say yes to the things that do

04/01: The big three

  • Control your perceptions - direct your actions properly - willingly accept what is outside your control

05/01: Clarify your intentions

  • Having an end in mind is no guarantee that you’ll reach it - no Stoic would tolerate that assumptions - but not having an end in mind is a guarantee you won’t
  • When your efforts are not directed at a cause or a purpose, how much will you know what do day in and day out? How will you know what to say no to and what to say yes to?

06/01: Where, who, what, and why

  • Have you taken the time to get clarity about who you are and what you stand for?

07/01: Seven clear functions of the mind

  • The proper work of the mind is the exercise of choice - to do and think right, refusal - of temptation, yearning - to be better, repulsion - of negativity of bad influences, of what isn’t true, preparation - for what lies ahead or whatever may happen, purpose - our guiding principle and highest priority and assent - to be free of deception about what’s inside and outside our control (and be ready to accept the latter).

08/01 - Seeing our addictions

  • Addiction is when we’ve lost the freedom to abstain
  • You must reclaim the ability to abstain because within it is your clarity and self-control

09/01 - What we control and what we dont

  • An honest understanding of what is within our control provides real clarity about the world: all we have is our own mind
  • That it’s much better and more appropriately directed inward

10/01 - If you want to be steady

  • It’s about filtering the outside world though the straightener of our judgement
  • If you want to be steady, if you want clarity, proper judgement is the best way

11/01 - If you want to be unsteady

  • Epictetus is reminding you that serenity and stability are results of your choices and judgement, not your environment
  • Your problem will follow you wherever you run and hide
  • If you seek to avoid the harmful and disruptive judgements that cause those problems, then you will be stable and steady wherever you happen to be

12/01 - The one path to serenity

  • Only thing you truly possess is your ability to make choices (and to use reason and judgement when doing so)
  • Sleep is a form of surrender and trust and how easily it comes

13/01 - Circle of control

  • A wise person knows what’s inside their circle of control and what is outside of it
  • According to the Stoics, the circle of control contains just one thing: your mind
  • There is clarity in simplicity

14/01 - Cut the strings that pull your mind

  • Every human being is pulled by these internal and external forces that are increasingly more powerful and harder to resist
  • As Viktor Frankl puts in The Will to Meaning: “Man is pushed by drives but pulled by values

15/01 - Peace is in staying the course

  • State of mind produces tranquility
  • Tranquility and peace are found in identifying our path and in sticking to is: staying the course, making adjustments here and there, naturally but ignoring the distracting sirens who beckon us to turn toward the rocks
